01Introduction to Microbiology

Microbiology is the study of organisms too small to see without magnification, including bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, and algae. The central concept to grasp first is that microorganisms are everywhere and most of them are not harmful. In fact, the majority play essential roles in nutrient cycling, food production, and human health. Only a small fraction are pathogenic (disease-causing).

Historical Foundations

Antonie van Leeuwenhoek was the first to observe microorganisms using his handcrafted microscopes in the 1670s, calling them "animalcules." Louis Pasteur disproved spontaneous generation with his famous swan-neck flask experiments and developed pasteurization. Robert Koch established the germ theory of disease and created Koch's postulates, a logical framework still used today to prove that a specific microbe causes a specific disease.

Key Point
Koch's postulates require four steps: (1) the microbe must be found in all diseased organisms but not healthy ones, (2) it must be isolated and grown in pure culture, (3) the cultured microbe must cause disease when introduced to a healthy host, and (4) the microbe must be re-isolated from the newly diseased host and shown to be the same organism.
Common Mistake
Students often confuse Pasteur and Koch. Remember: Pasteur disproved spontaneous generation and developed pasteurization and vaccines (rabies). Koch established the germ theory as a formal logical framework (Koch's postulates) and identified specific causative agents of anthrax and tuberculosis.

Domains of Life and Microbial Classification

All life is classified into three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. Bacteria and Archaea are both prokaryotic (no membrane-bound nucleus) but differ fundamentally in cell wall chemistry, membrane lipids, and ribosomal RNA sequences. Archaea often inhabit extreme environments (extremophiles) such as hot springs and salt lakes, though many also live in moderate environments.

Example
When you eat yogurt, you consume billions of Lactobacillus and Streptococcus thermophilus bacteria. These beneficial microbes ferment lactose into lactic acid, which thickens the milk and produces the tangy flavor.

Naming Microorganisms

Microbes are named using binomial nomenclature: the genus name is capitalized and the species name is lowercase, both italicized. After first use, the genus can be abbreviated. For example, Escherichia coli becomes E. coli. Modern classification relies heavily on 16S rRNA gene sequencing for prokaryotes and 18S rRNA for eukaryotes.

Worked Example: Applying Koch's Postulates
A physician notices that patients with a mysterious lung disease all have the same unusual bacterium in their sputum. How would you confirm this bacterium causes the disease?
1
Postulate 1 — Find it in disease, not health: Culture sputum from all sick patients AND from healthy controls. The bacterium must appear consistently in sick patients but not healthy ones.
2
Postulate 2 — Isolate it: Grow the bacterium alone on a culture plate to produce a pure culture. Confirm identity with staining and biochemical tests.
3
Postulate 3 — Re-create disease: Introduce the pure culture into a healthy animal model. If the same lung disease develops, you have strong evidence.
4
Postulate 4 — Re-isolate: Culture the sick animal's lungs, and confirm the same bacterium is recovered.
✓ Conclusion: Only if all four steps succeed can you claim this bacterium causes this disease.
Where Koch's Postulates Break Down
Some pathogens defy the postulates. Treponema pallidum (syphilis) cannot be cultured outside the body, so Postulate 2 fails. HIV cannot ethically be given to a healthy human volunteer, so Postulate 3 cannot be tested. For viruses like HIV, molecular techniques substitute for classic postulates. Knowing these exceptions is as important as knowing the rules.
Analogy: Pasteur vs. Koch
Think of Pasteur as the myth-buster (proved microbes come from other microbes, not thin air) and Koch as the detective (built the logical framework for proving which microbe commits the crime of causing a specific disease). Different jobs, different tools.
Concept Check
A scientist isolates a fungus from every patient with a skin rash and grows it in pure culture, but skips the animal inoculation step. Which of Koch's postulates did they fail to complete, and why does this matter?
They failed Postulate 3 (causing disease in a healthy host). Without this step, they cannot rule out that the fungus is simply an innocent bystander — a commensal colonizer present on sick skin for reasons unrelated to the rash. Correlation alone never proves causation.

02Prokaryotic Cell Structure

Bacterial cells are structurally simpler than eukaryotic cells, but they have unique structures that serve as targets for antibiotics and as tools for identification. Understanding bacterial anatomy is essential because many clinical and laboratory techniques exploit these structural differences.

Cell Shapes and Arrangements

Bacteria come in three primary shapes: cocci (spherical), bacilli (rod-shaped), and spirilla/spirochetes (spiral). Cocci can arrange as diplococci (pairs), streptococci (chains), staphylococci (grape-like clusters), or tetrads. Bacilli can appear as single rods, streptobacilli (chains), or coccobacilli (short, oval rods).

The Cell Envelope

The most clinically important structural distinction is between Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. The Gram stain exploits differences in cell wall thickness and composition.

Key Point
The Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) of Gram-negative bacteria is one of the most important molecules in microbiology. Its lipid A component is an endotoxin that triggers powerful immune responses and can cause septic shock.
Common Mistake
Students often say Gram-negative bacteria "have no peptidoglycan." They do have peptidoglycan, it is just much thinner. The key distinction is the presence of an outer membrane containing LPS.

External Structures

Flagella are long, whip-like appendages providing motility, powered by a proton motive force motor. Pili (fimbriae) are shorter projections used for attachment; the sex pilus (F pilus) is used during conjugation. Capsules are extracellular polysaccharide coatings that help bacteria evade phagocytosis and facilitate biofilm formation.

Endospores

Certain genera, most notably Bacillus and Clostridium, form endospores when nutrients become scarce. Endospores are the most resistant biological structures known, surviving boiling, UV radiation, desiccation, and most chemical disinfectants. Resistance comes from dipicolinic acid (DPA) complexed with calcium ions in the core, plus multiple protective coat layers.

Example
Clostridium botulinum endospores can survive improper home canning. When conditions become anaerobic in the sealed jar, the spores germinate and produce botulinum toxin. This is why pressure canning (121 degrees C) is required for low-acid foods.
Worked Example: The Gram Stain Step by Step
You receive a wound swab from a patient. Your job: determine whether the bacteria present are Gram-positive or Gram-negative so the physician can select the right antibiotic.
1
Crystal violet (primary stain): Apply to heat-fixed smear. ALL bacteria turn purple at this stage — both Gram-positive and Gram-negative.
2
Gram's iodine (mordant): Forms a crystal violet-iodine complex that gets trapped in thick peptidoglycan. Still, everything looks purple.
3
Alcohol/acetone (decolorizer): This is the critical step. Gram-positive bacteria: thick peptidoglycan retains the complex — stays purple. Gram-negative bacteria: the alcohol dissolves the outer membrane and thin peptidoglycan cannot retain the dye — loses color and becomes transparent.
4
Safranin (counterstain): Gram-negative bacteria (now colorless) absorb safranin and appear pink/red. Gram-positive (already purple) are unaffected.
✓ Purple = Gram-positive (thick peptidoglycan retained dye). Pink/red = Gram-negative (outer membrane + thin peptidoglycan lost dye). This guides antibiotic selection before culture results arrive.
Memory trick for decolorization: Think of Gram-positive bacteria as wearing a heavy wool sweater (thick peptidoglycan) that soaks up the crystal violet dye and refuses to let go. Gram-negative bacteria have only a thin undershirt (thin peptidoglycan) under a waterproof jacket (outer membrane) — when alcohol removes the jacket, the thin fabric can't hold the dye.
Clinical Application
A patient with a UTI has Gram-positive cocci in clusters on urinalysis smear. What does this tell you before culture results? Clusters suggest Staphylococcus. Gram-positive means: no LPS, no outer membrane barrier, so many Gram-positive-targeted drugs (penicillin class) could work. If the patient had Gram-negative rods instead, you'd think E. coli and consider a fluoroquinolone or nitrofurantoin while awaiting culture.
Concept Check
A student accidentally leaves the decolorizer on too long during a Gram stain. How would this affect the result, and which bacteria would be misidentified?
Over-decolorization would strip the crystal violet-iodine complex even from Gram-positive bacteria (whose thick peptidoglycan normally retains it). They would lose color and then pick up safranin — appearing pink/red like Gram-negatives. This would cause Gram-positive bacteria to be incorrectly reported as Gram-negative, potentially leading to wrong antibiotic selection. This is one reason Gram stain results should always be interpreted alongside patient history and culture.

03Bacterial Growth & Metabolism

Bacteria grow by binary fission, splitting one cell into two genetically identical daughter cells. Bacterial populations grow exponentially: a single E. coli dividing every 20 minutes could theoretically produce over 5 billion cells in just 11 hours.

The Bacterial Growth Curve

In fresh liquid medium, populations follow four phases. The lag phase is adjustment (synthesizing enzymes, not dividing). The log (exponential) phase is maximum division rate. The stationary phase is when growth rate equals death rate as nutrients deplete. The death (decline) phase is when cell death exceeds division.

Key Point
Generation time varies enormously. E. coli divides every ~20 minutes; Mycobacterium tuberculosis takes 15-20 hours. This explains why TB cultures must incubate for weeks.

Oxygen Requirements

ClassificationO2 RequirementExamples
Obligate aerobeRequires O2M. tuberculosis, Pseudomonas
Obligate anaerobeKilled by O2Clostridium, Bacteroides

Common Mistake
Facultative anaerobes CAN switch between aerobic and anaerobic metabolism (they prefer O2 for more ATP). Aerotolerant anaerobes ONLY ferment and simply tolerate oxygen without using it. These are not the same thing.

Metabolic Pathways

Aerobic respiration proceeds through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain (theoretical max ~38 ATP per glucose). In anaerobic respiration, the final electron acceptor is an inorganic molecule other than O2 (nitrate, sulfate). Fermentation uses an organic molecule as the final electron acceptor, producing ethanol, lactic acid, or mixed acids. Fermentation end products are diagnostically useful (e.g., the IMViC series: Indole, Methyl Red, Voges-Proskauer, Citrate).

Example
E. coli is methyl red positive and VP negative (mixed-acid fermentation). Enterobacter is methyl red negative and VP positive (butanediol fermentation). This distinction is used in the IMViC series for identification.
Worked Example: Identifying Oxygen Class from Clinical Clues
A lab technician places bacterial samples in four different culture conditions. Match each result to the correct oxygen classification.
1
Growth only on top of the tube (near the oxygen-rich surface): → Obligate aerobe (requires O₂, cannot survive without it). Example: M. tuberculosis — this is why TB loves the lung apex, which has the highest O₂ tension.
2
Growth only at the bottom of the tube (away from oxygen): → Obligate anaerobe (killed by O₂). Example: Clostridium — thrives in deep puncture wounds where O₂ cannot penetrate.
3
Growth throughout the tube, but denser at top: → Facultative anaerobe (prefers O₂ but survives without it). Example: E. coli.
4
Growth uniformly throughout, not denser at top or bottom: → Aerotolerant anaerobe (only ferments, indifferent to O₂). Example: Lactobacillus.
✓ The tube-growth pattern is a classic lab practical question. Key distinction: facultative anaerobes PREFER O₂ and grow denser near it; aerotolerant anaerobes are INDIFFERENT to O₂ and grow evenly.
Analogy: Growth Curve Phases
Think of bacteria starting a new job. Lag phase: orientation week — learning the ropes, no output yet. Log phase: peak productivity — doubling output every 20 minutes. Stationary phase: hit the supply limit — gains equal losses. Death phase: resources exhausted, people quit faster than they are hired.
Concept Check
Helicobacter pylori causes stomach ulcers and requires a microaerophilic environment to grow. If you culture it in normal air (21% O₂), what will happen, and what does this mean for growing it in the lab?
H. pylori will fail to grow (or grow very poorly) in normal atmospheric O₂ because it needs only 5–10% O₂ — much less than room air. In the lab, it must be incubated in special microaerophilic jars or gas-pak systems that reduce O₂ and elevate CO₂. This is one reason H. pylori cultures take 3–7 days and require special equipment — making it a notoriously difficult organism to grow.

04Microbial Genetics

Bacteria achieve genetic diversity without sexual reproduction through mutation and three mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer: transformation, transduction, and conjugation. Understanding these mechanisms explains how antibiotic resistance spreads so rapidly.

Bacterial Chromosome and Plasmids

Most bacteria have a single, circular chromosome in the nucleoid region. Many also carry plasmids: small, circular, self-replicating DNA carrying advantageous genes. R plasmids carry antibiotic resistance genes. F plasmids carry conjugation genes. Virulence plasmids carry toxin genes.

Horizontal Gene Transfer

MechanismHow It WorksKey Detail
TransformationUptake of free DNA from environmentCell must be "competent"; Griffith's experiment
TransductionPhage carries host DNA to new cellGeneralized (random) vs. specialized (near prophage site)
ConjugationDirect cell-to-cell transfer via pilusRequires F plasmid; Hfr cells have F integrated
Key Point
Conjugation is the most clinically significant mechanism because it transfers large DNA segments, including multiple antibiotic resistance genes on R plasmids, between even distantly related bacteria. This drives multidrug resistance in hospitals.
Common Mistake
Transformation, transduction, and conjugation are NOT forms of reproduction. Binary fission is reproduction. Horizontal gene transfer is about genetic diversity, not producing new cells.

Gene Regulation: The Operon Model

The lac operon is inducible: lactose metabolism genes are transcribed only when lactose is present AND glucose is absent. The trp operon is repressible: tryptophan synthesis genes are turned off when tryptophan is abundant. Catabolite repression ensures bacteria preferentially use glucose over other sugars.

Worked Example: Tracing an Antibiotic Resistance Outbreak
An ICU reports that a Gram-negative bacterium has suddenly become resistant to three different antibiotics at once. How did this happen so fast? No new mutations were detected.
1
Recognize the clue: Simultaneous multi-drug resistance appearing suddenly in a new species cannot come from point mutation alone — mutation is slow and typically changes one gene at a time. This points to horizontal gene transfer.
2
Identify the most likely mechanism: Three resistance genes transferred together suggests a plasmid (R plasmid) — a circular DNA element that carries multiple resistance genes at once. Conjugation is the most common route for R plasmid transfer in clinical settings.
3
Confirm with conjugation biology: The donor bacterium (carrying R plasmid) extended a sex pilus, made contact with a recipient cell in the ICU environment, and transferred the plasmid. The recipient gained all three resistance genes simultaneously.
4
Public health implication: Because plasmids replicate independently, every daughter cell of the new host also carries resistance. Strict contact precautions and antibiotic stewardship are the only ways to interrupt this cycle.
✓ This is exactly how hospital MDR outbreaks work. Conjugation transfers large DNA blocks (R plasmids) between even distantly related bacteria — far faster and more dangerous than mutation-driven resistance.
Worked Example: The Lac Operon Logic Puzzle
A bacterium has plenty of lactose available but also has high glucose. Will the lac operon be fully expressed?
1
Check the repressor: Lactose (converted to allolactose) binds and inactivates the lac repressor. The repressor is OFF — so the operon is de-repressed. This is a "yes" for transcription.
2
Check catabolite repression: High glucose means low cAMP. Low cAMP means the CAP activator protein cannot bind the promoter. Without CAP, RNA polymerase binds poorly. This is a "no" for full activation.
3
Net result: Operon is de-repressed but NOT fully activated. Only minimal, low-level transcription occurs. The bacterium is preferentially using glucose (easier to metabolize) and not fully committing to lactose metabolism.
✓ Both conditions must be met: lactose present (removes repressor) AND glucose absent (high cAMP activates CAP). This is the famous "glucose is preferred" catabolite repression — bacteria are strategically frugal about when they manufacture new enzymes.
Memory trick for HGT mechanisms: "Three ways to share genes without sex — Pickup (Transformation: pick up free DNA), Parcel Post (Transduction: phage delivers a DNA package), and Personal Handshake (Conjugation: direct pilus contact required)."
Concept Check
You add tryptophan to a bacterial culture. What happens to the trp operon, and how does this differ from how the lac operon responds to lactose?
The trp operon is a repressible operon. Adding tryptophan activates the trp repressor, which then binds the operator and turns OFF transcription of the tryptophan synthesis genes — because there is no need to make what is already abundant. This is the opposite logic from the lac operon: lactose addition removes its repressor and turns the operon ON. Inducible operons are turned on by the substrate; repressible operons are turned off by the end product.

05Viruses & Prions

Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites: packages of genetic material (DNA or RNA, never both) surrounded by a protein coat (capsid), sometimes with a lipid envelope. They are not considered living organisms by most biologists.

Viral Structure

Capsids come in three shapes: icosahedral (20-sided), helical (rod-shaped), and complex (like bacteriophages). Enveloped viruses acquire a lipid bilayer from the host during budding, with glycoprotein spikes for attachment. Enveloped viruses are more susceptible to disinfection because the lipid envelope is easily disrupted.

Viral Replication

Five steps: attachment to host receptor, penetration, synthesis of viral components using host machinery, assembly, and release (lysis or budding). Bacteriophages follow either the lytic cycle (immediate takeover and lysis) or lysogenic cycle (integration as prophage until triggered).

Key Point
The Baltimore classification groups viruses into seven classes by genome type: dsDNA, ssDNA, dsRNA, +ssRNA, -ssRNA, ssRNA-RT (retroviruses like HIV), and dsDNA-RT (hepadnaviruses like Hep B).

RNA Viruses and Retroviruses

Positive-sense ssRNA can be directly translated as mRNA (coronaviruses, poliovirus). Negative-sense ssRNA must first be transcribed by RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (influenza, Ebola, rabies). Retroviruses (HIV) use reverse transcriptase to make dsDNA that integrates as a provirus.

Common Mistake
Do not confuse lysogeny with latency. Lysogeny is phage DNA integrating into a bacterial chromosome. Latency is an animal virus (like herpes) persisting dormant in host cells. Different mechanisms, different organisms.

Prions

Prions are infectious agents composed entirely of misfolded protein (PrPSc), with no nucleic acid. They convert normal PrPC into the misfolded form, causing transmissible spongiform encephalopathies (CJD, mad cow disease, scrapie). Prions are extraordinarily resistant to heat, UV, and chemicals.

Worked Example: Identifying Viral Replication Strategy
An exam question tells you that a new virus has a negative-sense single-stranded RNA genome. Walk through what this virus must carry and why.
1
What does negative-sense mean? The RNA strand is the complementary/template strand — it reads opposite to mRNA. A ribosome cannot translate it directly as a message.
2
What must the virus bring along? It must package its own RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) inside the virion. Without this enzyme, the cell cannot convert the negative-sense genome into positive-sense mRNA that ribosomes can read.
3
Why can't the host provide RdRp? Human cells only transcribe DNA to RNA — they have no enzyme to make RNA from an RNA template. Only these viruses need to bring their own.
4
Real examples: Influenza, Ebola, rabies, RSV — all negative-sense ssRNA viruses that carry their own RdRp in the virion.
✓ Rule of thumb: Positive-sense RNA → goes straight to ribosome. Negative-sense RNA → must be transcribed first → virus must carry RdRp. Retroviruses (HIV) are different: ssRNA → reverse transcriptase makes DNA → integrates as provirus.
Worked Example: Lytic vs. Lysogenic Cycle Decision
A bacteriophage infects a rapidly dividing E. coli in a lab with abundant nutrients. Later, the same phage infects a starving E. coli. Would you expect the same replication outcome?
1
Scenario A — Healthy, well-fed host: The phage detects a healthy host with active transcription machinery. Lytic cycle is favored: take over the cell, replicate rapidly, burst out 100–200 new phages per cell.
2
Scenario B — Starving, stressed host: Lysed cells release phage into an environment with few new hosts. Better strategy: integrate as a prophage and "ride out" the hard times, replicating silently with the host chromosome.
3
The trigger for induction: When the dormant host later encounters DNA damage (UV light activates SOS response), the prophage senses impending host death, excises from the chromosome, and switches to the lytic cycle.
✓ Phages are strategic: lytic when conditions favor rapid spread, lysogenic when the environment is hostile. This is why lysogenic conversion (prophage carrying toxin genes like diphtheria or cholera toxin) is clinically significant — the toxin genes persist silently until conditions change.
Analogy: Retroviruses
HIV is like a spy who breaks into your house (the cell), rewrites your blueprints (reverse transcriptase makes DNA), files the new blueprints in your permanent records (integrates as provirus), and then waits. Every time your blueprints are copied for building work (cell division), the spy's instructions are copied too. You can evict the spy's furniture (antiretroviral drugs suppress replication) but you cannot purge the instructions from the permanent file (integrated provirus remains).
Concept Check
A healthcare worker accidentally gets stuck by a needle from a patient with hepatitis B. Hepatitis B is a dsDNA-RT virus (hepadnavirus). Why does it use reverse transcriptase if it already has double-stranded DNA?
Hepatitis B has an unusual "backwards" replication cycle. It transcribes its dsDNA into an RNA intermediate called pregenomic RNA (pgRNA) first, then uses reverse transcriptase to convert that RNA back into new dsDNA. This RNA→DNA step is the target of antiviral drugs like tenofovir, which inhibit the reverse transcriptase. The dsDNA form also explains why HBV can establish a nuclear cccDNA reservoir that is extremely difficult to eliminate — similar to the HIV provirus problem.

06Eukaryotic Microbes: Fungi, Protozoa & Helminths

Because eukaryotic pathogens share cellular machinery with human hosts, developing drugs that target them without harming the patient is much more challenging than developing antibacterials.

Fungi

Fungi have cell walls made of chitin. Yeasts are unicellular (reproduce by budding). Molds grow as branching hyphae forming a mycelium. Dimorphic fungi exist as mold at 25 degrees C and yeast at 37 degrees C: "mold in the cold, yeast in the beast."

TypeExamplesKey Details
Superficial/cutaneousDermatophytes (ringworm)Infect keratinized tissue

Protozoa

Unicellular eukaryotes classified by locomotion: amoebae (pseudopods), flagellates, ciliates, and apicomplexans/sporozoans. Major diseases include malaria (Plasmodium), giardiasis, amoebic dysentery, and toxoplasmosis.

Helminths

Parasitic worms with microscopic eggs/larvae: nematodes (roundworms like Ascaris, hookworm), cestodes (tapeworms), and trematodes (flukes like Schistosoma). Many have complex life cycles with intermediate hosts.

Worked Example: Using Dimorphism to Identify Fungal Infections
A 35-year-old farmer from the Ohio River Valley presents with fever, chest pain, and lung nodules on X-ray. Lab sends samples to both a room-temperature incubator (25 °C) and a body-temperature incubator (37 °C). The 25 °C plate shows fluffy mold colonies; the 37 °C plate shows small yeast cells. What does this tell you?
1
Apply the dimorphism rule: "Mold in the cold (25 °C), yeast in the beast (37 °C)." This organism is a dimorphic fungus.
2
Apply geographic clue: Ohio River Valley is the classic endemic zone for Histoplasma capsulatum. Infection comes from inhaling spores in soil contaminated with bird/bat droppings.
3
Confirm the disease mechanism: Inhaled mold conidia convert to yeast form at 37 °C inside macrophages. In immunocompetent hosts, infection is often mild or asymptomatic. In immunocompromised patients, it becomes a serious systemic infection.
4
Treatment approach: Itraconazole for mild-moderate; amphotericin B for severe disease. Antifungals target ergosterol in the fungal membrane — a molecule absent in human cells.
✓ Diagnosis: Histoplasmosis. The dimorphism finding + geography + occupational exposure (farm work, soil disturbance) are the classic clue trio on exams.
Why eukaryotic pathogens are harder to treat: Bacteria differ from us enough that antibiotics can safely target them (peptidoglycan, bacterial ribosomes). Fungi, protozoa, and helminths are eukaryotes — like us. They share our ribosomes, similar cell signaling, similar membranes. Every antifungal and antiparasitic drug is a careful balance between "kills the organism" and "doesn't poison the patient." This is why antifungal choices are limited and side effects can be serious.
Worked Example: Malaria Life Cycle (Simplified)
1. Anopheles mosquito injects sporozoites into bloodstream. 2. Sporozoites travel to liver — exoerythrocytic phase (asymptomatic). 3. Liver cells rupture, releasing merozoites into blood. 4. Merozoites invade RBCs — erythrocytic phase (causes fever/chills when RBCs rupture synchronously). 5. Some merozoites become gametocytes, taken up by another mosquito to complete the cycle. The fever pattern (every 48 or 72 hours) reflects synchronized RBC lysis. This timing is a classic exam clue for malaria.
Concept Check
An immunocompetent patient's blood culture grows Candida albicans. A colleague says "Candida is normal microbiota, so this must be a contaminated sample." Is the colleague right, and what clinical context would make you take this seriously?
The colleague is partially right that Candida colonizes skin and mucous membranes, but a blood culture growing Candida is never dismissed as contamination — it indicates invasive candidiasis, a serious and potentially fatal infection. This is most concerning in: (1) ICU patients with central venous catheters, (2) immunocompromised patients (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy), (3) patients on broad-spectrum antibiotics that wiped out normal bacterial competition. A positive blood culture for Candida always warrants treatment with antifungals (fluconazole or echinocandin).

07Microbial Control & Antibiotics

The central concept is selectivity: kill the pathogen without harming the host or the material being treated.

Common Mistake
Pasteurization is NOT sterilization. It reduces microbial load but does not kill all organisms, especially endospores. This is why pasteurized milk still expires.

Antibiotics: Mechanisms of Action

Five major mechanisms: (1) Cell wall synthesis inhibition (penicillins, cephalosporins, vancomycin). (2) Cell membrane disruption (polymyxins, daptomycin). (3) Protein synthesis inhibition at 30S (tetracyclines, aminoglycosides) or 50S (macrolides, chloramphenicol, clindamycin, linezolid). (4) Nucleic acid synthesis inhibition (fluoroquinolones target DNA gyrase; rifampin targets RNA polymerase). (5) Metabolic pathway inhibition (sulfonamides and trimethoprim block folic acid synthesis).

Key Point
Mnemonic: "Buy AT 30, CELL at 50." 30S: Aminoglycosides, Tetracyclines. 50S: Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin (macrolides), Linezolid, cLindamycin.

Antibiotic Resistance

Four mechanisms: enzymatic inactivation (beta-lactamases), target modification (altered PBPs in MRSA), decreased permeability (porin mutations), and efflux pumps. Resistance genes spread rapidly via plasmids and transposons through horizontal gene transfer.

Example
MRSA carries the mecA gene encoding PBP2a, an altered penicillin-binding protein with low affinity for ALL beta-lactams. Treatment options include vancomycin, daptomycin, and linezolid.
Worked Example: Choosing the Right Antibiotic Class
A 25-year-old develops a community-acquired pneumonia. Sputum Gram stain shows Gram-positive diplococci (suggesting S. pneumoniae). Walk through antibiotic selection logic.
1
Identify target: Gram-positive → no outer membrane, no LPS. Target: peptidoglycan cell wall synthesis. Best class: beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins) or vancomycin if resistant.
2
Check for likely resistance: Is this MRSA? No — S. pneumoniae is not MRSA. However, penicillin-resistant S. pneumoniae (PRSP) exists via altered PBPs. Local resistance rates matter.
3
Select specific drug: For community-acquired pneumonia, high-dose amoxicillin or a respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin). If atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila) are also suspected (they lack cell walls), add a macrolide — cell wall drugs won't touch them.
4
The conceptual point: Antibiotic selection = (1) identify the likely organism, (2) identify its key vulnerability, (3) select a drug targeting that vulnerability, (4) check for resistance mechanisms that could nullify the drug.
✓ This is the standard antibiotic reasoning framework for every clinical microbiology exam question. Organism → structural vulnerability → drug class → resistance check.
Worked Example: Why 100% Alcohol Does NOT Work Better Than 70%
A student thinks "more alcohol = better disinfection." She uses pure 100% ethanol instead of 70% isopropyl alcohol to wipe down a bench. Why is this actually less effective?
1
How alcohol kills bacteria: It denatures proteins. But protein denaturation requires water — water is the chemical catalyst for the denaturation reaction.
2
100% alcohol problem: Without water, the alcohol evaporates instantly before penetrating bacterial cells. It also forms a protective coagulated protein shell on the outside of the cell that prevents further penetration. Paradoxically, bacteria can survive inside this protective shell.
3
70% alcohol solution: The 30% water slows evaporation and facilitates protein denaturation throughout the cell. More contact time, better penetration, better kill.
✓ This is why 60–90% alcohol (not 100%) is the recommended range for antisepsis. More concentrated is not always more effective — mechanism matters.
Analogy: Antibiotic Mnemonic "Buy AT 30, CELL at 50"
Think of a two-story ribosome factory. The ground floor (30S subunit) is rented out to companies A and T: Aminoglycosides and Tetracyclines. The upper floor (50S subunit) has four tenants with the sign "CELL": Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin (macrolides), Linezolid, cLindamycin. Always capitalize the mnemonic letters when you write them so the pattern is visible.
Concept Check
A hospital epidemiologist notices that a Gram-negative rod is now resistant to carbapenems (last-resort beta-lactams). Investigation reveals no beta-lactamase gene is present. What other mechanism(s) could explain this resistance?
Without beta-lactamase, the remaining mechanisms are: (1) Porin mutations — carbapenems enter Gram-negative bacteria through outer membrane porins (OprD in Pseudomonas). Downregulation or loss of these channel proteins blocks drug entry. (2) Efflux pumps — upregulation of efflux pump systems (like MexAB-OprM in Pseudomonas) actively pumps the drug out before it can reach PBPs. (3) Altered PBPs — target modification reduces drug binding affinity. In clinical practice, carbapenem-resistant organisms (CROs) often combine multiple mechanisms simultaneously, making them extremely difficult to treat.

08Innate & Adaptive Immunity

Innate immunity is fast, non-specific, and present from birth. Adaptive immunity is slower but highly specific and creates immunological memory.

Innate Immunity

First line: physical barriers (skin, mucous membranes), chemical barriers (lysozyme, stomach acid), normal microbiota. Second line: phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), NK cells, complement, inflammation, fever. Toll-like receptors (TLRs) recognize PAMPs such as LPS (TLR4), flagellin (TLR5), and dsRNA (TLR3).

Key Point
Complement has three outcomes: opsonization (C3b coats pathogens), membrane attack complex (C5b-C9 forms pores), and inflammation (C3a/C5a recruit immune cells). All three activation pathways converge at C3.

Adaptive Immunity

B cells produce antibodies (humoral immunity). Helper T cells (CD4+) coordinate responses via cytokines. Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) kill infected host cells.

Common Mistake
Serum abundance order is GAMED (IgG > IgA > IgM > IgE > IgD), but IgM is always produced FIRST in a primary response. On re-exposure, IgG dominates the faster secondary response.

Vaccines

Types: live attenuated (MMR, varicella), inactivated (flu shot, Hep A), subunit/conjugate (Hep B, HPV), toxoid (tetanus, diphtheria), and mRNA (COVID-19). Live attenuated produce the strongest response but are contraindicated in immunocompromised patients.

Worked Example: Reading an Immune Response Timeline
A patient receives their first tetanus vaccine. Three weeks later, serum antibody levels are measured. Four months later, they receive a booster. Describe what happens to antibody levels at each stage and which immunoglobulin class is dominant.
1
First vaccine (primary response): Lag period of ~1–2 weeks while B cells recognize antigen and undergo clonal selection. IgM rises first. After class switching (with T cell help), IgG appears. Peak is modest. Some cells become long-lived memory B cells and plasma cells.
2
Three weeks post-vaccine: IgG is the dominant class now. Levels are detectable but may not yet provide full protection — this is why the full series matters.
3
Booster (secondary response): Memory B cells recognize antigen immediately. Response is faster (days, not weeks), larger (10–100x more antibody), and longer-lasting. IgG dominates with higher affinity due to somatic hypermutation in germinal centers.
4
Why IgM first? IgM is the default immunoglobulin — B cells make it before class switching occurs. High IgM = recent/active infection. High IgG = past infection or vaccination. This is how serology tests distinguish current from past exposure.
✓ Primary response: slow, low titer, IgM then IgG. Secondary response: fast, high titer, IgG dominant, higher affinity. This is the immunological memory that vaccines exploit.
Worked Example: Complement Pathway Identification
An exam question describes a patient who lacks MBL (mannose-binding lectin). Which complement pathway is impaired? What diseases is this patient susceptible to, and will the other pathways compensate?
1
Identify the pathway: MBL = mannose-binding lectin = the Lectin pathway. This pathway recognizes mannose residues on microbial surfaces (common on bacteria, fungi) without needing antibodies.
2
What bacteria does MBL normally catch? Encapsulated bacteria with mannose-rich capsules, common bacteria before specific antibodies are made (early infection), and certain fungi.
3
Compensation by other pathways: The alternative pathway can still activate spontaneously on bacterial surfaces. Once antibodies are made, the classical pathway activates. So MBL deficiency mainly creates a window of vulnerability early in infection before antibodies form.
4
Clinical result: Increased susceptibility to recurrent bacterial infections in children (when antibodies are not yet established). MBL deficiency is surprisingly common (~5% of people) and often mild.
✓ All three pathways converge at C3. Loss of one pathway weakens but doesn't abolish complement. The most critical complement deficiencies clinically are C5-C9 (cannot form MAC) — these patients are highly susceptible to Neisseria infections.
Memory trick for IgG crossing the placenta: "IgG = 'Goes' through the placenta." It's the only antibody class that does, providing passive immunity to newborns for ~6 months. IgM is too large (pentamer). IgA goes to breast milk instead. On every exam, if they mention maternal antibodies protecting a newborn, the answer is IgG.
Concept Check
A 28-year-old pregnant woman who never had chickenpox (varicella) needs protection. Can she receive the live attenuated varicella vaccine? If not, what are the options?
No — live attenuated vaccines are contraindicated in pregnancy because the attenuated (weakened but living) virus could theoretically infect and harm the fetus. This includes MMR, varicella, and yellow fever vaccines. Options: (1) Wait until after delivery to vaccinate. (2) Varicella-zoster immune globulin (VZIG) provides immediate passive immunity if she is exposed during pregnancy — pre-formed antibodies without a live virus. (3) After delivery, vaccinate before leaving the hospital. This also illustrates the passive vs. active immunity distinction: VZIG is passive (borrowed antibodies, short-lived), vaccination is active (own immune response, long-lasting).

09Infectious Disease & Epidemiology

Every infectious disease requires a chain of infection: reservoir, portal of exit, mode of transmission, portal of entry, and susceptible host. Breaking any link prevents disease spread.

Transmission Modes

ModeDescriptionExamples
Direct contactPerson-to-personSTIs, staphylococcal skin infections
DropletLarge respiratory dropletsInfluenza, pertussis
AirborneSmall suspended particlesTB, measles, chickenpox
Fecal-oralContaminated food/waterHepatitis A, cholera, norovirus
Vector-borneArthropod transmissionMalaria, Lyme, plague

Virulence Factors

Adhesins allow attachment. Capsules inhibit phagocytosis. Exotoxins are secreted proteins: A-B toxins (diphtheria, cholera), membrane-disrupting toxins (hemolysins), and superantigens (TSST causing cytokine storm). Endotoxin (lipid A of LPS) is released when Gram-negative bacteria lyse.

Key Point
Exotoxins: secreted proteins, specific effects, can be toxoided, from Gram+ and some Gram-. Endotoxin: lipid A of LPS, general inflammatory effects, cannot be toxoided, only Gram-, heat-stable.
Worked Example: Breaking the Chain of Infection
Three weeks after a nursing home outbreak of influenza, the infection control team reviews what interventions worked and why. Map each intervention to the link in the chain of infection it broke.
1
Vaccination of staff and residents: Breaks the "susceptible host" link — vaccinated individuals cannot become infected even if exposed. This is the most upstream intervention.
2
Isolating symptomatic residents in their rooms: Breaks the "portal of exit" and "mode of transmission" links — the infected person cannot shed virus to others in common spaces.
3
Masking healthcare workers around symptomatic patients: Breaks the "portal of entry" link — masks prevent droplets (large respiratory particles from influenza) from entering the healthcare worker's airways.
4
Hand hygiene after patient contact: Breaks the "mode of transmission" link — prevents contact transmission of influenza from contaminated surfaces.
✓ Exam principle: every infection control question maps to the chain of infection. Ask yourself: which link is being targeted? Multiple simultaneous interventions are more effective because breaking any single link is sufficient to stop spread.
Worked Example: Exotoxin Mechanism Identification
A patient has watery, voluminous, non-bloody diarrhea after eating contaminated rice (Vibrio cholerae). Explain the molecular mechanism step by step.
1
Toxin type: Cholera toxin is an A-B toxin. B subunit binds GM1 ganglioside on intestinal epithelial cells. A subunit is internalized and enters the cell.
2
Molecular sabotage: A subunit ADP-ribosylates (permanently modifies) the Gs-alpha protein, locking it in the "on" position. Gs-alpha permanently stimulates adenylyl cyclase.
3
Downstream effects: Adenylyl cyclase makes massive amounts of cAMP. Elevated cAMP activates protein kinase A, which opens CFTR chloride channels in the epithelium. Cl⁻ floods into the gut lumen.
4
Why "rice-water" diarrhea: Chloride movement is followed osmotically by Na⁺ and water. Up to 20 liters per day can be lost. Death from dehydration, not from the bacteria itself — which never invades tissues.
✓ Treatment: aggressive oral rehydration (WHO oral rehydration solution). Antibiotics (doxycycline) shorten duration but hydration saves lives. The bacteria never invades — it just hijacks cAMP signaling from the gut lumen.
A-B toxin trick: Think B = Binding (gets you in the door), A = Active damage (does the actual harm). Other A-B toxins: diphtheria toxin (ADP-ribosylates EF-2, stopping protein synthesis), pertussis toxin (ADP-ribosylates Gi-alpha, blocking inhibitory G proteins), anthrax toxin (adenylyl cyclase directly). The ADP-ribosylation chemistry appears in every major A-B toxin — once you see this pattern, you recognize the whole family.
Concept Check
Measles is airborne (transmitted by tiny droplet nuclei that remain suspended in air). Influenza spreads by larger respiratory droplets. Why does this difference mean measles requires N95 respirators but influenza only requires surgical masks for healthcare workers?
Surgical masks block large respiratory droplets (>5 µm) that fall quickly and only travel ~3–6 feet — sufficient for influenza. Airborne particles from measles are tiny droplet nuclei (<5 µm) that remain suspended in room air for hours and can travel across entire rooms through ventilation systems. A surgical mask cannot filter these tiny particles. N95 respirators are specifically designed to filter ≥95% of airborne particles ≥0.3 µm. This is the same reason tuberculosis (also airborne) requires N95 respirators and negative-pressure isolation rooms to prevent recirculation of contaminated air.

Diagnostic Methods

Microscopy (Gram stain, acid-fast stain) provides rapid preliminary identification. Culture on selective/differential media remains the gold standard. Biochemical tests (catalase, coagulase, oxidase, IMViC) narrow identification. Molecular methods (PCR, 16S rRNA sequencing) provide rapid, specific identification. Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion and broth microdilution (MIC) guide antibiotic selection.

Example
Distinguishing Staph from Strep starts with catalase. Staphylococci: catalase-positive (bubbles with H2O2). Streptococci: catalase-negative. Within Staph, coagulase separates S. aureus (positive) from coagulase-negative species like S. epidermidis.

Biofilms

Structured communities in an extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) matrix attached to surfaces. Bacteria in biofilms are 100-1,000x more resistant to antibiotics. They form on medical devices, teeth, and wounds. Quorum sensing (cell-density-dependent communication using autoinducers) coordinates biofilm formation and virulence.

Key Point
The normal microbiota provides colonization resistance by competing with pathogens, produces antimicrobial substances, and trains the immune system. Disrupting it (e.g., broad-spectrum antibiotics) enables opportunistic infections like C. difficile and Candida overgrowth.
Worked Example: Identifying an Unknown Gram-Positive Coccus
A wound culture grows Gram-positive cocci in grape-like clusters. Walk through the biochemical identification tree.
1
Gram stain + morphology: Gram-positive cocci in clusters → Staphylococcus species (chains would suggest Streptococcus). Confirm with catalase test.
2
Catalase test: Add H₂O₂ to a colony smear. Bubbles = catalase positive = Staphylococcus. No bubbles = catalase negative = Streptococcus (or Enterococcus).
3
Coagulase test (for Staph): Mix with rabbit plasma. Clot forms = coagulase positive = S. aureus (the dangerous one: abscess-forming, endocarditis, TSS). No clot = coagulase negative = S. epidermidis, S. saprophyticus, or other CoNS.
4
If CoNS: novobiocin sensitivity can distinguish further. S. saprophyticus (UTI in young women) is novobiocin-resistant. S. epidermidis (implant infections) is sensitive.
✓ The identification tree: Gram+ cocci clusters → catalase → coagulase → novobiocin. Each test narrows the differential. This same logic applies to any unknown isolate.
Worked Example: Understanding a C. difficile Infection
A 70-year-old hospitalized patient completes a 10-day course of clindamycin for pneumonia. Three days later, she develops 8–10 watery stools per day with severe cramping. Stool tests positive for C. difficile toxins. Explain the pathophysiology.
1
Why clindamycin? Clindamycin (50S inhibitor) is strongly associated with C. difficile colitis because it disrupts colonic flora particularly effectively. C. difficile spores persist in the hospital environment and are highly resistant to alcohol hand sanitizer (alcohol does NOT kill spores — only soap and water physically removes them).
2
Loss of colonization resistance: Normal gut flora (especially Bacteroides, Bifidobacterium) compete with C. difficile for nutrients and produce substances that inhibit it. When clindamycin kills these competitors, C. difficile spores germinate and proliferate unchecked.
3
Toxin damage: C. difficile produces Toxin A (enterotoxin, causes fluid secretion) and Toxin B (cytotoxin, destroys colonocytes). Toxin B is more potent and the primary diagnostic target. Together they cause pseudomembranous colitis — a visible pseudomembrane on colonoscopy.
4
Treatment: Oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin (fidaxomicin preferred to reduce recurrence). Ironically, metronidazole used to be first-line but recurrence rates were high. Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) is used for recurrent cases to restore normal flora.
✓ This case illustrates: antibiotic stewardship matters (clindamycin was broad enough to kill normal flora), spore transmission (contact precautions + soap and water), and the microbiome's protective role. Every piece of the colonization resistance concept is visible in this one case.
Worked Example: MacConkey Agar Interpretation
MacConkey agar selects for Gram-negatives (crystal violet + bile salts kill Gram-positives) and differentiates by lactose fermentation. Pink/red colonies = lactose fermenters (acid turns pH indicator red): E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter. Colorless colonies = non-fermenters: Salmonella, Shigella, Pseudomonas. If a stool culture from a patient with bloody diarrhea grows colorless colonies, you think Salmonella or Shigella first. If pink colonies with metallic green sheen = E. coli on EMB agar. Each selective/differential medium is designed around one or two distinguishing biochemical traits.
For clinical microbiology exam questions, use this 5-step approach: (1) Note the patient's immune status. (2) Identify exposure history and geography. (3) Read the Gram stain result carefully. (4) Apply the biochemical test tree. (5) Match to the most likely pathogen before selecting treatment. Rushing to treatment without completing steps 1–4 is the most common reason students pick wrong answers.
Concept Check
A 35-year-old cystic fibrosis patient is hospitalized for a pulmonary exacerbation. Sputum culture grows a Gram-negative rod producing a blue-green pigment. Which organism is this, why is it especially dangerous in CF patients, and what makes it difficult to treat?
Pseudomonas aeruginosa, identified by its characteristic blue-green pigment (pyocyanin). It thrives in CF patients because: (1) the thick mucus in CF airways creates a protected low-oxygen environment that Pseudomonas can colonize, (2) once established, it switches to a mucoid biofilm-producing phenotype that is virtually impossible to eradicate from the airways. It is intrinsically hard to treat because: (1) low outer membrane permeability reduces antibiotic entry, (2) it has potent efflux pumps, (3) it produces AmpC beta-lactamase, (4) biofilms confer 100–1000x resistance. Treatment typically requires two antipseudomonal drugs (combination therapy) to reduce resistance emergence. Once chronic colonization is established in CF, the goal shifts from cure to exacerbation control.
